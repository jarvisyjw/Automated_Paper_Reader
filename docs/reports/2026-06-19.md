### 1. Geometry-Preserving in 3D Gaussian Splatting for LiDAR-Camera Extrinsic Calibration

- **Score:** 4.55/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20103

#### The Problem
Targetless LiDAR-camera calibration using 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) often prioritizes photometric rendering quality, causing the proxy geometry to drift from the true LiDAR structure (geometric decay). This metric distortion severely limits the accuracy of multi-modal perception and extrinsic calibration in autonomous driving.

#### The Solution
The paper proposes a framework that preserves the metric geometry of the Gaussian proxy by aggregating multi-view LiDAR observations for dense depth supervision and explicitly blocking photometric gradients from updating the Gaussian spatial parameters.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Multi-view LiDAR point clouds and synchronized camera images; outputs optimized LiDAR-camera extrinsic parameters and a metrically accurate 3DGS scene.
- **Backbone or representation:** 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) acting as a differentiable geometric proxy.
- **Core modules:** Dense Depth Anchoring (DDA) and Gradient Decoupling (GD).
- **Training or optimization:** DDA uses multi-view LiDAR for dense depth supervision; GD blocks photometric gradients from updating spatial parameters (position, scale, rotation) while allowing opacity and spherical harmonics to optimize for rendering.
- **Inference or deployment:** Used offline or during system initialization to calibrate multi-sensor rigs without manual targets.
- **Important design choices:** Decoupling the gradient flow ensures that the rendering loss does not corrupt the geometric anchoring provided by LiDAR.

#### Results
Validated on public driving datasets (KITTI). The method consistently outperforms existing targetless calibration methods in extrinsic accuracy, demonstrating that preserving metric geometry directly translates to better calibration.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Photometric optimization in standard 3DGS inherently distorts metric geometry, making it unsuitable for precise calibration without constraints.
- **Finding 2:** Gradient decoupling successfully isolates the rendering appearance from the geometric structure.
- **Finding 3:** Multi-view LiDAR aggregation provides a robust dense depth prior that anchors the Gaussians to physical space.

#### Strengths
Directly addresses multi-sensor calibration and 3D Gaussian representations. The insight into preserving metric geometry in 3DGS during joint optimization is highly valuable, technically sound, and practically deployable for autonomous driving rigs.

#### Limitations
Relies on the availability of multi-view LiDAR sweeps to build the dense depth anchor, which may be computationally heavy or difficult in highly dynamic scenes where LiDAR points cannot be cleanly aggregated.

#### Relevance To My Research
This is a paper to read deeply. It directly aligns with my priorities in multi-sensor calibration, 3D Gaussian representations, and robust autonomous driving deployment.

#### Actionable Follow-Up

### 2. VisDom: Sparse Novel View Synthesis with Visible Domain Constraint

- **Score:** 4.45/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20531

#### The Problem
Sparse novel view synthesis (NVS) with NeRF and 3DGS often overfits when supervision is limited, producing floating artifacts and inconsistent geometry. Standard silhouette consistency regularizers are insufficient because silhouette-consistent regions can extend beyond the true object geometry.

#### The Solution
VisDom is a learning-free geometric constraint that augments visual hull reconstruction by enforcing a minimum multi-view visibility requirement. It defines a "visible domain" (space observed by at least $K$ views) to restrict volumetric sampling and Gaussian placement.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Sparse input images and their corresponding silhouettes; outputs high-quality 3D NeRF or 3DGS models with reduced floaters.
- **Backbone or representation:** Implicit (NeRF) and explicit (3DGS) radiance fields.
- **Core modules:** Visible Domain Constraint (filtering 3D space based on multi-view visibility count $K$).
- **Training or optimization:** Restricts volumetric sampling in NeRF and guides Gaussian placement/cloning in 3DGS during standard photometric optimization.
- **Inference or deployment:** Applied during the training/optimization phase of sparse-view 3D reconstruction.
- **Important design choices:** Learning-free, domain-agnostic, and requires only silhouettes, introducing no learned parameters.

#### Results
Evaluated on three challenging datasets (including Omni3D and MipNeRF360). VisDom enables high-quality object-centric reconstruction from as few as four input images. Applying it on top of GaussianObject matches or surpasses it at 22x lower training cost.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Silhouette consistency alone is too weak a prior for sparse views; multi-view visibility count is a much stronger spatial constraint.
- **Finding 2:** VisDom drastically reduces floating artifacts and improves geometric coherence in both NeRF and 3DGS.
- **Finding 3:** The method significantly reduces training cost by preventing the optimization from wasting capacity on empty or ambiguous space.

#### Strengths
Highly relevant to sparse 3D reconstruction and 3DGS. It provides a simple, actionable, and mathematically grounded geometric prior that is easily pluggable into existing pipelines.

#### Limitations
Requires accurate silhouettes as input, which can be challenging to obtain automatically in complex, cluttered, or low-contrast real-world robotic environments.

#### Relevance To My Research
This is a method to reproduce. It is highly relevant to feed-forward 3D reconstruction and sparse-view mapping scenarios in robotics.

#### Actionable Follow-Up

### 3. HilDA: Hierarchical Distillation with Diffusion for Advancing Self-Supervised LiDAR Pre-training

- **Score:** 4.35/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20189

#### The Problem
Current camera-to-LiDAR knowledge distillation approaches treat Vision Foundation Models (VFMs) as black-box teachers, relying on frame-wise feature similarity. This fails to exploit the teacher's layer-wise semantic structure, global context, and the rich spatiotemporal information inherent in LiDAR sequences.

#### The Solution
HilDA is a self-supervised pretraining framework for LiDAR backbones that combines hierarchical distillation (multi-layer and global context) with a temporal occupancy diffusion objective to promote spatiotemporal consistency.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** LiDAR sequences and synchronized camera images; outputs a pre-trained LiDAR backbone with rich semantic and geometric features.
- **Backbone or representation:** 3D LiDAR backbone (e.g., voxel or point-based) and 2D Vision Foundation Models.
- **Core modules:** Multi-layer distillation, global context distillation, and temporal occupancy diffusion objective.
- **Training or optimization:** Progressive semantic alignment across VFM layers, scene-level global context matching, and a diffusion-based loss that enforces spatiotemporal consistency in 3D occupancy.
- **Inference or deployment:** The pre-trained backbone is fine-tuned for downstream autonomous driving tasks (3D detection, scene flow, occupancy).
- **Important design choices:** Using a diffusion objective to model the geometric "where" alongside the semantic "what" from the VFM.

#### Results
Achieves state-of-the-art results on cross-modal distillation benchmarks and outperforms prior distillation approaches on 3D object detection, scene flow, and semantic occupancy prediction.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Layer-wise VFM alignment captures progressive semantic abstraction better than single-layer distillation.
- **Finding 2:** Temporal occupancy diffusion effectively regularizes the LiDAR representation, enforcing physical and geometric consistency over time.
- **Finding 3:** Combining semantic distillation with geometric diffusion yields a more robust 3D world model.

#### Strengths
Highly relevant to autonomous driving deployment, multi-sensor perception, and 3D world modeling. The integration of diffusion for geometric priors is technically novel and practically deployable.

#### Limitations
The temporal occupancy diffusion objective likely adds significant computational overhead during the pre-training phase compared to standard feature matching.

#### Relevance To My Research

#### Actionable Follow-Up

### 4. MMD-SLAM: Structure-Enhanced Multi-Meta Gaussian Distribution-Guided Visual SLAM

- **Score:** 4.25/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19874

#### The Problem
Existing 3DGS-based Visual SLAM systems fail to fully exploit underlying structural information (like lines and planes), which limits rendering quality, causes geometric inconsistencies, and reduces tracking robustness in structured environments.

#### The Solution
MMD-SLAM leverages the Atlanta World (AW) assumption to guide a Multi-Meta Gaussian representation. It incorporates point-line fusion for pose optimization and explicitly encodes structural priors into the Gaussian evolution strategy.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Monocular or RGB-D image sequences; outputs camera poses and a structurally consistent 3DGS map.
- **Backbone or representation:** Multi-Meta Gaussian representation (encoding points, lines, and surfaces with dominant directions).
- **Core modules:** Point-line fusion for tracking, Atlanta World structural prior extraction, and structure-aware Gaussian evolution.
- **Training or optimization:** Joint optimization of camera poses (using point and line reprojection errors) and Gaussian parameters (regularized by AW dominant directions).
- **Inference or deployment:** Runs online as a visual SLAM system for robot navigation and mapping.
- **Important design choices:** Explicitly modeling lines and planar structures alongside point-based Gaussians to constrain the map in textureless or repetitive areas.

#### Results
Achieves a 48.56% reduction in ATE RMSE on ScanNet and a 5.71% improvement in PSNR on Replica compared to MonoGS, demonstrating state-of-the-art tracking and mapping.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Point-line fusion significantly improves tracking robustness in low-texture environments.
- **Finding 2:** The Atlanta World assumption provides a strong, computationally tractable prior for indoor and urban structured scenes.
- **Finding 3:** Multi-meta Gaussians prevent the "blobby" artifacts common in standard 3DGS SLAM.

#### Strengths
Highly relevant to 3DGS-based SLAM and explicit 3D representations. The integration of structural priors into Gaussian primitives offers a novel way to improve mapping consistency.

#### Limitations
The Atlanta World assumption may degrade in highly unstructured, natural, or organic environments where orthogonal or dominant planar structures are absent.

### 5. SHED Light on Segmentation for Dense Prediction

- **Score:** 4.2/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://openreview.net/forum?id=gsRlLeHiiI

#### The Problem
Most dense prediction models (like monocular depth) predict each pixel independently, producing blurred boundaries and geometrically inconsistent surfaces, which harms downstream 3D perception and robotics tasks.

#### The Solution
SHED is an encoder-decoder that builds an explicit hierarchy of image segments and decodes it by unpooling coarse tokens back to fine ones through learned assignments, rather than generic feature upsampling.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Single RGB image; outputs dense predictions (monocular depth, optical flow) with sharp boundaries.
- **Backbone or representation:** Hierarchical segment tokens (explicit hierarchy of image segments).
- **Core modules:** Segment-grouping encoder and assignment-inverting decoder.
- **Training or optimization:** Trained end-to-end from the dense prediction objective alone, without requiring explicit segmentation labels.
- **Inference or deployment:** Feed-forward inference for real-time depth or flow estimation in robotic pipelines.
- **Important design choices:** Unpooling via learned segment assignments preserves sharp occlusion boundaries and intra-object coherence.

#### Results
Sharpens occlusion boundaries in monocular depth, transfers strongly from synthetic to real domains, and uses less computation than the comparable DPT baseline. Also improves optical flow baselines.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Decoding a learned segment hierarchy is a general principle for dense prediction, not just segmentation.
- **Finding 2:** Explicit hierarchical grouping organizes features by scene layout, improving semantic alignment and 3D reconstruction.
- **Finding 3:** SHED achieves better boundary sharpness and domain transfer than standard DPT with lower compute.

#### Strengths
Directly addresses monocular depth estimation with a novel structural prior. Highly relevant for feed-forward 3D perception pipelines and robust spatial reasoning.

#### Limitations
The hierarchical grouping mechanism might struggle with highly textured, repetitive, or fractal patterns where semantic segment boundaries are ambiguous.

#### Relevance To My Research
This is a paper to read deeply. It directly impacts my work on monocular depth estimation and feed-forward 3D reconstruction.

#### Actionable Follow-Up

### 6. CalTennis: Large Multi-View Tennis Video Dataset and Benchmark of Monocular-to-3D Pose Estimation

- **Evidence level:** `pdf`

#### The Problem

#### The Solution
The authors introduce CalTennis, a massive multi-view dataset (11M frames, 51 hours), and propose a label-free evaluation protocol using multi-view consistency to benchmark depth and foot contact accuracy.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Monocular video frames; outputs 3D human pose and foot contact predictions.
- **Backbone or representation:** Standard monocular-to-3D pose estimation models.
- **Core modules:** Multi-view geometric consistency metrics (footwork, stability) and automated video calibration/synchronization.
- **Training or optimization:** Not a training method; focuses on evaluation and benchmarking.
- **Inference or deployment:** Used as an offline benchmark for evaluating 3D pose algorithms.
- **Important design choices:** Using multi-view epipolar and temporal consistency as a proxy for ground-truth depth and contact evaluation.

#### Results
Benchmarks SOTA methods, revealing that while 3D joint angle recovery is accurate, all models struggle significantly with absolute depth and foot contact consistency.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Multi-view consistency is a viable, label-free metric for evaluating monocular 3D depth and contact.
- **Finding 2:** Current SOTA models fail to maintain physical plausibility (foot contact) in dynamic, in-the-wild scenarios.
- **Finding 3:** Large-scale synchronized multi-view data exposes failure modes hidden in standard single-view benchmarks.

#### Strengths

### 7. Tri-Info: Generalizable, Interpretable Failure Prediction for VLA Models via Information Theory

- **Score:** 3.3/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19998

#### The Problem
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are black boxes whose physical interactions can cause irreversible harm. Detecting failures in a generalizable, interpretable way across sim-to-real gaps is essential for safe deployment.

#### The Solution
Tri-Info formalizes VLA control as a closed-loop information pipeline and derives Triple Information-theoretic signals (action diversity, temporal consistency, state-coupling) to detect failures without retraining.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** VLA rollout states and actions; outputs failure probabilities and interpretable diagnostics.
- **Backbone or representation:** Information-theoretic metrics (entropy, mutual information) applied to VLA policy outputs.
- **Core modules:** Tri-Info signal extraction (diversity, consistency, coupling).
- **Training or optimization:** Training-free; relies on analytical information-theoretic properties of the rollout.
- **Inference or deployment:** Runs online as a safety monitor alongside the deployed VLA policy.
- **Important design choices:** Using information theory to capture the systemic breakdown of the closed-loop control rather than just visual out-of-distribution detection.

#### Results
Evaluated across six VLA models and three environments. Achieves 83% accuracy on real-world tasks, transferring across architectures and the sim-to-real gap without retraining.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Successful and failed rollouts carry systematically different information-theoretic signatures.
- **Finding 2:** Information-theoretic failure detection generalizes across the sim-to-real gap better than learned visual anomaly detectors.
- **Finding 3:** Tri-Info provides interpretable diagnostics (e.g., loss of action diversity vs. decoupling from state).

#### Strengths
Highly useful for robust robot deployment and monitoring navigation/VLA policies. Offers a simple, training-free way to detect out-of-distribution failures.

#### Limitations
It only monitors and detects failures; it does not inherently correct the policy or provide a recovery mechanism.

#### Relevance To My Research

- **Evidence level:** `pdf`

#### The Problem
Thermal infrared (TIR) imaging suffers from severe stochastic and fixed-pattern noise, especially indoors, which breaks downstream estimation tasks like visual odometry and monocular depth estimation.

#### The Solution
TIDY is a lightweight wavelet-domain denoiser trained on real clean-noisy TIR data. It explicitly disentangles noise from structural content using novel Wavelet Entropy and Wavelet Directional Stripe Index losses.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Noisy TIR images; outputs denoised TIR images.
- **Backbone or representation:** Wavelet-domain CNN.
- **Core modules:** Wavelet Entropy loss and Wavelet Directional Stripe Index loss.
- **Training or optimization:** Supervised training on real clean-noisy TIR pairs using the proposed wavelet losses.
- **Inference or deployment:** Runs online at ~34Hz as a plug-and-play preprocessing module for robotics.
- **Important design choices:** Operating in the wavelet domain to explicitly target stripe artifacts and stochastic noise without blurring spatial structures.

#### Results
Improves robustness and yields consistent gains in downstream robotics tasks, including thermal inertial odometry and monocular depth estimation, in severe indoor and zero-shot settings.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Wavelet domain processing explicitly disentangles fixed-pattern stripe noise from structural content.
- **Finding 2:** Denoising TIR images significantly improves the accuracy of downstream geometric tasks like depth estimation.
- **Finding 3:** The method is lightweight enough for real-time edge deployment (~34Hz).

#### Strengths

- **Evidence level:** `pdf`

Autonomous vehicles must satisfy conflicting requirements (safety, comfort, traffic rules) under multimodal prediction uncertainty. Standard planners often fail to prioritize these constraints correctly in safety-critical scenarios.

#### The Solution
An uncertainty-aware trajectory planning framework that incorporates a predefined lexicographic ordering over Signal Temporal Logic (STL) specifications, solved via Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Ego state, surrounding traffic predictions; outputs safe, optimized trajectory.
- **Backbone or representation:** Signal Temporal Logic (STL) and Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control.
- **Core modules:** Lexicographic STL ordering, Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) for uncertainty handling.
- **Training or optimization:** Non-smooth optimization via MPPI sampling, prioritizing safety STL constraints over comfort constraints.
- **Inference or deployment:** Runs online as the trajectory planner for autonomous driving.
- **Important design choices:** Using lexicographic ordering to ensure safety constraints are never compromised by lower-priority comfort objectives.

#### Results
Demonstrated in simulation scenarios, showing efficient handling of conflicting objectives under realistic multi-modal uncertainty.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Lexicographic ordering prevents safety constraints from being mathematically compromised by comfort objectives.
- **Finding 2:** CVaR effectively integrates multimodal prediction uncertainty into the STL evaluation.
- **Finding 3:** MPPI can successfully optimize non-smooth, priority-ordered STL specifications.

#### Strengths
Mathematically sound safety guarantees under uncertainty. Highly relevant to safe autonomous driving deployment.

#### Limitations

### 10. Towards 3D karst underwater scene reconstruction from rotating sonar data

- **Score:** 2.75/5
- **Evidence level:** `pdf`
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20322

#### The Problem
Mapping underwater karst aquifers is challenging due to sparse, noisy sonar data and severe SLAM drift, which limits standard 3D reconstruction methods.

#### The Solution
A pipeline combining continuous-time SLAM for trajectory drift correction with a two-stage deep learning method (nPSR, POCO) for surface reconstruction from sparse sonar point clouds.

#### How It Works
- **Inputs and outputs:** Rotating sonar profiler data; outputs a navigable 3D mesh of underwater conduits.
- **Backbone or representation:** Continuous-time SLAM and neural implicit surface reconstruction (nPSR, POCO).
- **Core modules:** Continuous-time trajectory optimization, deep learning point cloud meshing.
- **Training or optimization:** SLAM optimizes the continuous trajectory; DL models are pre-trained or fine-tuned to mesh sparse sonar points.
- **Inference or deployment:** Offline processing for hydrogeological analysis and immersive visualization.
- **Important design choices:** Using continuous-time SLAM to handle the specific motion distortions of rotating sonar in underwater environments.

#### Results
Produces an immersive and navigable 3D mesh for hydrogeological analysis, correcting severe trajectory drift.

#### Key Findings
- **Finding 1:** Continuous-time SLAM effectively corrects motion distortion and drift in sparse sonar data.
- **Finding 2:** Deep learning meshing (nPSR/POCO) can hallucinate plausible surfaces from extremely sparse sonar point clouds.
- **Finding 3:** The pipeline enables 3D reconstruction in environments where visual and LiDAR sensors fail completely.

#### Strengths
Addresses an extreme domain (underwater sonar) with a complete SLAM-to-mesh pipeline.

#### Limitations
